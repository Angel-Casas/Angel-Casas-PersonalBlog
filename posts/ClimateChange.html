A light in the dark

No amount of experimentation can ever prove me right; a single experiment can prove me wrong.

Albert Einstein

The Scientific Method, the method by which science is carried out, involves building hypothesis based on previous observations about a phenomenon (induction, in other words, going from the specific observation to the more general idea), the study of the implications of this hypothesis and its effects on reality (deduction, in other words, going from the general idea to the specific observation)and the experimental testing of this hypothesis which must be verifiable and repeatable/replicable by anyone following the same steps. This method allows for predictions to appear as a consequence of studying a phenomenon and the successive discarding of information that is erroneous or not corresponding with reality. It is this predictions which can then be tested for it's ability to explain the phenomenon studied. If we can successfully predict an event from a given initial hypothesis through experiment we say we have a new scientific truth, which will be once again replaced by a new hypothesis which can explain reality with greater precision.

The influence of the sun

As the IPCC correctly points out: Global climate is determined by three primary influences on the planet; firstly is the solar energy recieved from the sun which is largely affected by changes in orbit of the earth around the sun; followed secondly by the solar energy reflected back out of the planet, due to the high reflection of polar ice caps - known as the albedo effect -, aerosols and clouds/land cover; and lastly by the effects of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere which can trap more of the solar energy inside the planet instead of reflecting it. Most of the data gathered around global temperatures and gas composition of the atmosphere comes from different sources such as ice cores, tree rings, ocean sediments, coral reefs and layers of sedimentary rock. All these sources point towards a warming of the planet, something which has happened before many times and will continue to happen, for the earth continually moves through cycles of ice ages and global warming, the key questions are if the anthropogenic effects on the climate are causing the rise in temperatures or if it's effects are negligible in contrast to the effects of the sun.

In the image we can appreciate how the rise in sea level has an almost perfect correlation with the incident solar flux in our oceans, a fact which shows us with absolute clarity how the sun does indeed have measurable effects on the planet's climate. So even though the IPCC doesn't seem to like the evidence linking solar activity and cosmic rays to global warming, the effects and the correlations with global warming are much higher than the models based on increases in carbon dioxide. In the same way NASA argues that the Milankovitch cycles cannot explain the rise in temperatures providing as only evidence the fact that during the last 40 years solar activity has decreased, something which for them confirms that the rise in temperatures must be atributed to the rise in carbon dioxide emisions. The problem with this argument lies in not taking into account that a reduction in solar activity during a relatively small time period does not necessarily mean that temperature must go down as a consequence, rather that the rate of increase of the temperature will decrease inasmuch as solar activity continues to decrease, something which helps understand how temperatures can keep going up despite having a reduction in solar activity.

An extraordinary friend of life

Let's now focus our attention on Carbon Dioxide, the greenhouse gas which has attracted most of the media attention and seems to be the culprit of all the evil and warming of our planet. Carbon Dioxide, being formed by a carbon atom and two oxygen atoms joined together by a covalent bond, sits at the base of all life cycles on earth; being used by the plant kingdom as a source of energy and as a prerequisite to producing growth and fruiting bodies; and by the animal kingdom as an essential compound in order to sustain celular life. Biological lifeforms throughout history have reduced the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere and increased it's oxygen content, allowing more complex lifeforms to benefit from this increasing richness in oxygen. Carbon is associated with most lifeforms and it's effect in the cycle of life is crucial. This means a big variation in the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere can have unpredictable consequences in many lifeforms on earth. Being such an important compound we must point out the benefits of such an extraordinary friend of life which, generally keeps getting portrayed as an obstacle or an intruder in the wellness of life on this planet.

As we can read from the graph, during the latter part of the Carboniferous, the Permian and the first half of the Triassic period, roughly 250-320 million years ago, the concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere was half what it is today while the temperature was 10ºC higher than today. If we look at the Cretaceus period towards the Eocene, roughly 35 to 100 million years ago, a decrease of carbon dioxide was accompanied by high temperature values. Scientists have also noted that an increase in carbon dioxide was always preceded by an increase in temperature decades or centuries in advance, something which can be explained when we understand that oceans act as carbon sinks, storing most of the carbon dioxide in it's underground cold currents, and increased solar radiation reaching the oceans end up warming and melting ice caps, which in return transfer new concentrations of cold water downwards thereby releasing the trapped carbon dioxide upwards to the atmosphere.

Drawing on this point about the vital cycles which occur in our oceans lets see how marine phytoplankton can help regulate the carbon dioxide and oxygen levels on the atmosphere and the oceans through the cycle known as the biological pump. As atmospheric carbon dioxide is absorbed by the oceans, marine phytoplakton use it in addition to sunlight (solar energy) to photosynthesize, producing a range of chemical compounds and structural polymers as well as oxygen in the process and help to remove about one-third of the atmospheric carbon dioxide.

Outside of the oceans, green biomass achieves the same Biosequestration(The capacity to capture and store the greenhouse gases) in order to enhance the life of the Plant and Animal kingdom, interchanging carbon dioxide for oxygen. Oxygen is the most abundant gas in the atmosphere at 21% by volume, whereas carbon dioxide fills only 0.04% (400ppm) of the atmosphere gases by volume and although this may seem a small amount, it is precisely carbon dioxide the main reason we have these oxygen levels and a flourishing of life in the biosphere.

According to a new study published in the journal Nature, many of the earth's vegetated lands (from a quearter to half the total land cover) have shown significant greening over the last 35 years largely due to rising levels of carbon dioxide.

Another study published by NASA has recently shown how increased carbon dioxide levels actually increase crop yields for many agricultural farms due to an increased rate of photosynthesis which consequently reduces the amount of water lost through transpiration on the leaves, something which aligns with the findings all around the world of a general increase in crop yields over the last decades. Although an increase in carbon dioxide seems to improve the growth of certain plants, it is clear that an increase in temperature is detrimental for the soils and can cause more drawbacks due to increased exposure to heat waves or less moisture of soil. The effects of increased temperatures can still be counteracted by a more controlled environment and by keeping good soil composition, allowing plants to achieve greater rates of yields while maintaining a high nutritional intake from the waters and the soil.

The effects of global temperature on human civilizations has been long studied by many anthropologists and paleoclimatologists such as Brian Fagan, who more recently published a book titled The Great Warming in which he studies the climate from the Holocene until our recent times. In the book he takes a detailed look into the effects of the medieval warm period, a term coined by paleoclimatologist H. Lamb, starting somewhere around 800 A.C with a progressive rise in temperatures until approximately 1200 A.C, where average temperatures rose by more than one degree causing a surplus of harvest for many farmers and peasants. This period of time is characterized by being a benign period with respect to climate catastrophes, something which took a turn to worse during the little ice age which spanned from the 16th Century up until the 19th Century. As we can see, our planet has had periods of global warming followed by periods of decreases in temperatures and glaciations and in general humanity and the natural world has been better off during periods of warmer temperatures, where a surplus of life seems to dominate the planet. Although increases in temperature can also damage various ecosystems and propiciate a desertification of certain landscapes, it is in our hands to investigate the overall effects of this changes and keep studying the implications of increases in temperatures and carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere, something which may help us in preventing damages to our soil and crop yields in the future to come.
